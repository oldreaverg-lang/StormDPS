How to Use StormDPS

Getting Started

Pick any storm from the sidebar, or type a name (like "Katrina") and its year. Click Visualize to see its full analysis. Use the timeline slider to step through the storm's life and watch the wind field change on the map.

What the Scores Mean

Every storm gets three scores from 0 to 100. Here's how to read them:

Destructive Power Score (DPS)

80-100Catastrophic — Evacuate if in path
60-79Extreme — Major damage likely
40-59Severe — Significant threat
20-39Moderate — Prepare if in forecast cone
10-19Minor — Stay informed
0-9Minimal — Low concern

Impact Area Score (IAS)

Measures how geography and rainfall amplify actual damage beyond wind alone (coastal shelf shape, rainfall, stall risk).

Economic Risk Score (ERS)

Measures economic exposure along the storm path (property values, infrastructure density in the threat zone).

Why not just use Categories?

The Saffir-Simpson scale only measures peak wind speed at the center. It ignores the storm's size, which is often the biggest factor in damage. Hurricane Ike (2008) made landfall as "only" a Cat 2, but its enormous wind field caused catastrophic damage across the entire Texas coast.

DPS captures what the Category scale misses: size, surge, duration of coastal exposure, and geographic breadth.

Technical terms glossary
  • IKE (TJ) — Integrated Kinetic Energy in Terajoules. Total wind energy accounting for both speed and storm size.
  • mph — Miles per hour (wind speed).
  • mb / hPa — Millibars, a pressure unit. Lower = stronger storm. Normal sea-level is ~1013 mb.
  • kt — Knots (1 kt = 1.15 mph). Used for storm movement speed.
